Pigeon

A Physical Facebook Machine

This project explored the ways people treat different forms of communication and conducted various experiment with users, in order to create a new experience using their online data. It ended with Pigeon, a concept product that allows a user to experience Facebook through a more personal analog interaction, in an attempt to change how they value their online communication.

Read the Project Journal

Research

For this project I was tasked with using a users data to create a new experience, that would change how users perceived and interact with that data.

I initially started by looking at online and offline forms of communication, and especially at how users don't feel a certain level of commitment or responsibility for messages they don't reply to. Many users said they often don't reply to messages straight away or even forgot, hence they didn't feel jilted when a friend didn't reply to there message.

From here I decided to look into my own messages and see if anything interesting could be learnt by looking at the messages I had sent on Facebook that people had read and not replied to. (I also decided to make a concept website that used this data in a fun and thoughtful way. ) I put up all 82 messages I had sent in the last year on a wall and invited people to write on post it notes why they thought I had not received a reply.

The results lead me to realising that a lot of the messages I had sent didn't warrant a reply; It was easy to understand from looking at all of them that most didn't demand answer, and thus easy for people to forgot about. I myself realised that I wouldn't feel guilty for not replying to a lot of the messages. From here I decided to look at ways to make to let users have a more interesting, meaningful and engaging way to use Facebook messenger.

Development

As part of my research, I interviewed many people to discover the unwritten rules of these different forms of offline and online communication. For example, letters turned out to be one of the most scripted and therefore less honest forms of communication. Users all said they loved receiving letters and knowing a person had taken the time to write it. However, they also said when they wrote letters, they used a very strict format and tended to write in more considered and less blunt fashion. In contrast, a lot of users said that they used Facebook messenger in a much more fluid and forthright way.

I decided it would be interesting to mix these mediums, so for a day I was allowed access to 2 users Facebook messages. I instructed them not to go on the website and to delete the app for a day. I then presented them with their messages, printed in handwritten fonts in an envelope. Even though the users knew they were printed by me, they really felt touched by the experience. One user summed up the test nicely saying "Every message meant more, the sad ones were sadder, the funny ones funnier and the boring ones, even duller."

From here I decided to do one more test, I took over another user's Facebook, but this time live. I hand delivered all of their messages, notifications and event invites. They were also able to interact with the facebook in an analogue way. For example, circling attending on an invite to RSVP, or writing on the back of a message to send a handwritten picture message reply.

Like the first 2 users, this one felt very touched by all of the deliveries, and also felt much more compelled to respond instantly. One comment she did make was that there was a lot of waste, particularly in the form of notifications she wasn't interested in and group messages that she wasn't invested in.

From here I started to look at how I could compile all of this feedback into a product that would engage users with Facebook in a more analogue, hence engaging, way.

Outcome

The outcome of this project was Pigeon, a device that let users interact with Facebook's main functions with pen and paper. Pigeon works as a users Facebook answering machine. As new Facebook messages or notifications arrive, Pigeon's arm will raise, users can then take delivery of their printed mail.

During my research, many people spoke of ignoring messages and notifications purely because they weren't interested in the first place. So as well as replying to messages or RSVPing to events, the user's had the ability to unsubscribe from notification threads as easily as putting a cross through a printed letter.

Users could also receive links as letters by printing these links as QR codes, these could then be scanned by the recipient in their own time. This was a play on the traditions of giving a friend a tape to listen to.
